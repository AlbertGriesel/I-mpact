PROJECT SPECIFICATION
AI Environmental Impact Tracker
Working app name: Sprout or I/mpact
A friendly, AI-powered platform for measuring, understanding, planning, and 
reducing personal environmental impact.
Core promise
Make environmental impact understandable and actionable. Ask only for information that 
meaningfully improves accuracy, use measured data whenever possible, and help users turn their 
results into realistic weekly action.
Primary metrics: net water consumption, net electricity consumption, and net carbon footprint (CO2e).

1. Overview
The goal of this project is to build an AI-powered environmental impact tracker that enables 
anyone to calculate, understand, compare, plan, and reduce their personal environmental 
footprint.
The application should be free, easy to use, optimistic, and designed for the general public, with a 
particular focus on South African and broader African households. A user with no environmental 
knowledge should be able to complete an assessment in less than 10 minutes while still receiving a 
realistic estimate of their impact.
The application should reflect local realities such as municipal utilities, prepaid meters, 
loadshedding, backup generators, solar inverters, mixed household energy sources, and regional 
differences across South Africa and Africa.
The application measures three primary environmental metrics:
 Net water consumption
 Net electricity consumption
 Net carbon footprint (CO2e)
The objective is not only to calculate a footprint. The application should educate users, show where 
their impact comes from, make the results relatable, track progress over time, create a sense of 
community, and provide practical AI-guided support for improvement.
2. Core Principles
2.1 Simplicity and meaningful inputs
Users should answer as few questions as possible. Every input must pass a relevance test:
 Ask the question only if the answer significantly improves the accuracy of the calculation, 
changes the recommendation, or is required for a core feature.
 Do not ask for information that can reasonably be inferred, calculated, looked up from an 
approved dataset, or extracted from an uploaded bill.
 Use progressive disclosure: estimation questions should only appear when measured data is 
unavailable.
 Avoid duplicated inputs across water, electricity, and carbon sections when one answer can be 
reused.
2.2 Accuracy
Whenever possible, calculations should be based on measured data instead of estimates.
 Utility bills, meter statements, prepaid records, and direct consumption values are preferred 
over behavioural estimates.
 Vehicle databases are preferred over asking users to know fuel efficiency.
 Airport databases should be used to calculate flight distance instead of asking users to estimate 
it.

 Regional electricity, water, and emissions datasets should be used where trustworthy local data 
is available.
 If data is missing, use trustworthy sources such as government agencies, utilities, universities, 
research institutions, and established scientific datasets.
2.3 Accessibility
The application should be suitable for people with little environmental knowledge. Questions and 
explanations should use simple language, and unfamiliar concepts should include short, optional 
explanations.
2.4 Transparency
Users should understand how their footprint was calculated, which inputs were measured or 
estimated, and which activities contribute most to their impact. The application should not present 
uncertain estimates as exact facts.
2.5 Optimism and agency
The tone should be friendly, practical, and encouraging without hiding poor performance. The 
product should make clear that individual action is not the only solution to environmental 
problems, but that personal choices can still reduce resource use, save money, influence 
households and communities, and contribute to broader changes in demand and norms.
3. Product Identity and Design Language
The final product name is undecided. The two current options are:
 Sprout - friendly, organic, simple, and growth-oriented.
 I/mpact - modern and reflective, emphasizing both personal impact and the possibility of 
change.
The interface should use a consistent visual language:
 Primary palette: light blue, green, white, and soft grey.
 Friendly rounded cards, clear spacing, approachable illustrations, and simple iconography.
 A fun and lightly playful design without making the scientific results feel childish.
 Optimistic microcopy and progress feedback. Avoid guilt-heavy or fear-based messaging.
 Nature-inspired motion and visuals that react to the user's current impact score.
4. Overall User Journey
1. Open the app and understand its purpose from the home page.
2. Create an account or sign in.
3. Choose account privacy: public or private.
4. Choose an assessment method: guided questionnaire or AI conversation.
5. Provide measured environmental data, upload bills, or answer fallback estimation questions.
6. Calculate water, electricity, and carbon metrics using a deterministic calculation engine.
7. View the feedback dashboard with KPIs, graphs, comparisons, explanations, and weekly goals.

5. Accounts, Privacy, and Profiles
5.1 Authentication
Users should be able to create an account and sign in securely. Environmental assessments, goals, 
historical results, achievements, and streaks should be linked to the user account.
5.2 Public or private account
Users should be able to choose whether their account is public or private and should be able to 
change this preference later.
 Private account: personal results and profile statistics are visible only to the user.
 Public account: approved profile statistics can appear on the profile page, Explore page, 
leaderboard, and real-life stories page.
 Public status must be based on explicit consent. Sensitive raw data, uploaded bills, exact 
household details, and private conversations must never be exposed publicly.
 Users should be able to withdraw public consent, after which their public profile and related 
social appearances should be hidden.
5.3 Profile page
Each user should have a profile page. It should display:
 Display name and optional profile image.
 How long the user has been on the app.
 Current impact score or rating.
 Water, electricity, and carbon performance summaries.
 Current weekly or monthly streak.
 Achievements and badges earned.
 Goals completed and recent progress.
 For public users only: the subset of statistics approved for public display.
6. Two Assessment Methods
The application should allow users to complete the assessment in one of two ways. Both methods 
must collect the same underlying data and produce identical calculations and final results.
6.1 Method 1 - Guided Questionnaire
The application asks structured questions using forms, sliders, dropdowns, checkboxes, and file 
uploads. This method is intended for users who prefer a traditional questionnaire.

6.2 Method 2 - AI Conversation
The user chats naturally with an AI assistant. The assistant should ask only relevant follow-up 
questions until enough information has been collected. It should extract structured values from 
natural-language answers and store them in the same data schema used by the guided 
questionnaire.
Important requirement
The conversational assistant must not ask every possible question. It should use the same conditional 
logic as the questionnaire and stop once the minimum meaningful data has been collected.
7. General Information
Collect only the general information required to choose appropriate regional factors and 
normalize household data:
 Household size.
 Country.
 Province, state, or region - optional unless needed for a regional factor.
 Municipality or local utility - optional unless needed for bill interpretation or local factors.
Household-level water and electricity values should be divided or otherwise normalized 
appropriately when presenting per-person impact. The app should clearly distinguish household 
totals from personal estimates.
8. Water Assessment
8.1 Preferred option - bill or statement upload
Users should be able to upload an image or digital copy of a municipal water bill or water 
statement. The image-processing system should extract relevant fields, including:
 Billing period or statement dates.
 Measured water consumption, preferably in litres or kilolitres.
 Water bill amount, where available.
 Meter readings, where useful for validation.
 Municipality or utility name, where it can be identified reliably.
The user must be shown the extracted values and be allowed to correct them before calculations 
are finalized. Actual measured consumption should always take priority over bill-cost conversion 
or behavioural estimates.
8.2 Manual entry
If no bill is uploaded, allow the user to enter monthly water consumption directly. A monthly 
water bill amount may be accepted as a fallback only when a defensible tariff or conversion 
method is available.

8.3 Rainwater harvesting
Ask whether the household uses rainwater harvesting. If yes, ask for the approximate percentage 
of household water supplied by rainwater. Rainwater should be treated as having a significantly 
lower environmental burden than municipally supplied water, using a transparent assumption or 
factor.
8.4 Estimation questions - only when needed
Only when reliable consumption data is unavailable, ask a minimal set of questions such as:
 Average shower duration.
 Number of showers per week.
 Garden irrigation.
 Swimming pool ownership.
Additional behavioural questions should only be added if testing shows that they materially 
improve the estimate.
9. Electricity Assessment
9.1 Preferred option - bill, receipt, or meter record upload
Users should be able to upload images of electricity bills, prepaid electricity receipts, or meter 
statements. The AI extraction system should identify relevant values such as:
 Billing or purchase period.
 Electricity consumption in kWh, when shown.
 Electricity bill or prepaid purchase amount.
 Tariff, meter, municipality, or utility information when reliably identifiable.
 Multiple prepaid purchases within a period, where the user uploads more than one receipt.
The app should verify and normalize units, show extracted values for user confirmation, and 
prioritize measured kWh over monetary conversion.
9.2 Manual entry
If no bill or receipt is available, allow users to enter monthly electricity consumption directly. 
Monthly bill amount may be used only as a fallback where a suitable tariff method is available.
9.3 Renewable electricity
Ask whether the household generates renewable electricity. Options include Solar, Wind, and 
None. If renewable electricity is used, estimate the percentage of household electricity supplied by 
renewable sources.
9.4 Backup power and loadshedding
Ask whether the household uses backup power during outages. Options include Generator, 
Inverter and battery, Solar battery backup, UPS, and None.

The carbon footprint should include transport, household energy, flights, heating, diet, food waste, 
shopping or consumption habits, backup power, and offsets where data is available.
10.1 Flights
Allow users to add multiple flights. For each flight collect:
 Departure airport.
 Arrival airport.
 Cabin class.
 One-way or return.
 Number of trips per year.
Flight distance should be calculated automatically from airport coordinates or an approved flight-
distance data source.
10.2 Personal vehicle
 Vehicle manufacturer.
 Vehicle model.
 Model year.
 Annual distance driven.
 Average number of passengers.
The application should determine fuel type and fuel economy automatically whenever a reliable 
vehicle database is available. Manual fuel economy should be a fallback.
10.3 Public transport
Collect transport type and average distance travelled each week. Supported transport types should 
include Bus, Train, Minibus taxi / taxi, Subway, and Other.

10.4 Home heating
Collect primary heating method, months used annually, and average hours used per day. Heating

10.5 Diet
Allow users to choose the description that best matches their diet: Heavy meat eater, Average diet,

11. Artificial Intelligence and API Requirements
Artificial intelligence serves four distinct roles. These roles should be separated from the 
deterministic calculation engine.
11.1 Conversational data collection
Use an LLM through an API. The API should receive the current structured assessment state plus 
the conversation and return structured data for any newly collected values, missing fields that still 
matter, and the next concise follow-up question when needed.
 Use structured JSON output or function/tool calling where supported.
 Validate extracted values against the application schema before saving them.
 Do not allow the LLM to invent consumption values when the user has not supplied enough 
information.
 Keep API keys server-side or in environment variables/secrets. Never expose keys in client code 
or the repository.
 Provide clear fallback behaviour if the API is unavailable.
11.2 Environmental analysis
After deterministic calculations are complete, send the calculated results and relevant user context 
to the LLM API. The AI should:
 Explain the user's environmental footprint in simple language.

 Identify the largest contributors.
 Explain why those contributors matter.
 Recommend practical improvements.
 Prioritize recommendations by likely impact, feasibility, and cost or effort.
 Estimate the environmental benefit of each recommendation where the calculation engine can 
support a defensible estimate.
 Avoid making unsupported claims or presenting uncertain estimates as exact.
11.3 AI planning assistant
The application should include a dedicated planning chat where users can discuss how to improve 
their impact. This feature should use an LLM API and should be grounded in the user's current 
results, historical progress, goals, and stated constraints.
 The user can ask questions such as how to reduce water use without changing their garden, 
how to lower electricity use on a budget, or how to plan lower-carbon transport for the week.
 The assistant should translate broad intentions into specific, measurable, realistic actions.
 The assistant should help revise weekly goals when circumstances change.
 The assistant should not directly overwrite user data or goals without explicit confirmation.
11.4 AI image extraction for bills and receipts
Use a multimodal AI model or document-extraction API capable of interpreting uploaded images. It 
should extract relevant values from water bills, electricity bills, prepaid receipts, and statements.
 Return extracted fields in a strict structured schema.
 Preserve the original image privately and do not expose it on public profiles.
 Show confidence or flag uncertain fields when the provider supports it.
 Require user review and correction of extracted values before final submission.
 Use traditional parsing or OCR rules where more reliable for a known bill format; the AI model 
should not be the only possible extraction path.
Architecture rule
The LLM is an advisor and interpreter, not the source of truth for arithmetic. Environmental 
calculations must use established conversion factors and deterministic code so the same inputs 
produce the same outputs.

them to read long technical documents. It should be visually engaging and divided into three clear 
topics.
12.1 Greenhouse gases
 Explain what greenhouse gases are and what CO2e means.
 Explain the main personal sources covered by the app, including energy, transport, flights, diet, 
and consumption.

12.2 Electricity
 Explain the difference between electricity consumption and the emissions associated with

 Explain why grid mix, renewable energy, efficiency, peak use, backup generation, and

13. Feedback Dashboard and Progress Tracking
After completing an assessment, users should receive a clear feedback dashboard rather than a 
single score.
13.1 Core KPI cards
The dashboard should show separate KPI-style cards for:
 Water consumption.
 Electricity consumption.
 Carbon footprint.
Each card should show the current value, comparison with the previous period or previous 
assessment, and comparison with an appropriate benchmark where available.
13.2 Historical comparison
Users should be able to compare their current impact with previous statistics. The dashboard 
should show change over time and make direction clear, for example percentage improvement or 
deterioration since the previous assessment and since the user joined the app.

13.3 Separate graphs for each metric
Provide separate trend graphs for water, electricity, and carbon. Do not combine unrelated units 
on one axis. Graphs should support weekly, monthly, and longer-term views when enough 
historical data exists.
13.4 Unit period dropdown
Users should be able to choose whether statistics are displayed per day, week, month, or year. The 
selected period should consistently update cards, graphs, breakdowns, and comparisons where 
mathematically appropriate.
13.5 Relevant and relatable comparisons
In addition to exact values, the dashboard should generate relatable comparisons. Examples 
include:
 Your annual water use is roughly equivalent to the volume of a small swimming pool.
 Your electricity use this month could power a typical efficient refrigerator for approximately a 
certain period.
 Your annual carbon footprint is comparable to a certain number of return trips on a relevant 
route, where the comparison is based on a defensible factor.
These comparisons must be based on defined conversion factors and should be selected for 
relevance and scale. Avoid absurd comparisons that are technically true but not useful.
13.6 Benchmarks and context
Where reliable data is available, compare results against South African, African, country-level, and 
global averages. The app should clearly label the benchmark source and geographic scope. A 
benchmark should not be shown if the underlying comparison is misleading or unsupported.
13.7 Specific weekly goals
Feedback should include a small number of specific weekly goals based on the largest realistic 
improvement opportunities. Goals should be measurable and practical, for example:
 Reduce shower time by a total of 15 minutes across the week.
 Replace two short car trips with walking, cycling, or public transport.
 Reduce geyser heating time or adjust its schedule where appropriate.
 Plan one lower-impact meal change that fits the user's existing diet rather than prescribing an 
unrealistic full lifestyle shift.
The AI may help phrase and personalize goals, but the expected environmental saving should 
come from deterministic calculations where possible.

Users should be encouraged to keep their data current. During onboarding or settings, users should

15. Community, Stories, and Leaderboard
15.1 Real-life stories
Create a page that shows real-life stories of users who reduced their impact through the app. 
Stories may only be shown when users have consented to a public account and separately 
consented to having their story featured.
 Show the starting challenge, actions taken, time period, and measured improvement.
 Prefer verified in-app statistics over purely anecdotal claims.
 Allow users to share practical lessons and realistic obstacles.
 Do not expose sensitive household or billing information.
15.2 Explore and public profiles
A public Explore page should allow users to discover public profiles, progress stories, and 
achievements. It should focus on inspiration and practical change, not only competition.
15.3 Leaderboard
Provide a public ranking system for users who opt in. Rankings should be designed carefully so 
users with naturally different household circumstances are not unfairly compared.
 Possible ranking views: improvement percentage, goal completion, streak length, and impact 
score.
 Prefer improvement-based rankings over raw lowest-consumption rankings when this creates 
a fairer comparison.
 Allow filters such as country or region only when privacy and sample size are sufficient.
 Public users should control which approved summary statistics appear.
16. Mascot and Reactive Environmental Visuals
16.1 Mascot concept
The app should include a single daisy flower growing on top of a small Earth. This mascot visually 
reflects the user's current environmental impact relative to appropriate national averages or 
benchmarks.
 Better-than-average impact: the Earth is clear and bright, the sky is sunny, colours are healthy, 
and the daisy stands upright and grows.
 Near-average impact: the scene remains mostly healthy with mild clouds or reduced vibrancy.

17. Achievements, Goals, and Streaks
17.1 Achievements
Users should unlock achievements when they complete meaningful actions or maintain good 
performance. Examples include:
 First assessment completed.
 First verified bill uploaded.
 Completed all weekly goals.
 Reduced one metric for three consecutive reporting periods.
 Reached a personal best impact score.
 Maintained a long update streak.
 Reduced a major footprint contributor by a meaningful percentage.
Achievements should reward actual progress and consistent engagement rather than encourage 
meaningless clicking or data entry.
17.2 Streak feature
Track how many consecutive weeks or months a user has kept their profile updated or completed 
the required check-in. The streak cadence should match the user's selected reminder cadence.
 Weekly users build a weekly streak.
 Monthly users build a monthly streak.
 Streak status should appear on the profile page and dashboard.
 A missed update should not necessarily erase all historical recognition; preserve best streak as 
well as current streak.

18. Calculation Philosophy
The application should calculate environmental impact using established conversion factors and 
scientific datasets. Calculations must be deterministic and reproducible.
 Measured data always takes priority over estimated data.
 Behavioural estimates should only be used when measured values are unavailable.
 Where possible, use South African and African regional datasets, including local electricity grid 
factors, municipal water data, transport assumptions, and country-specific emissions factors.
 Store factor source, geography, unit, and version or date where practical so calculations can be 
audited and updated.
 The app should distinguish between direct consumption metrics and environmental impact 
metrics. For example, kWh is not the same thing as electricity-related CO2e.

20. Data and State Requirements
The application should maintain structured data so that questionnaire inputs, chat inputs, image 
extraction, calculations, history, goals, and social features all use consistent records.
 User record: account, privacy status, join date, reminder cadence, and unit-period preference.
 Assessment record: timestamp, reporting period, source type for each input, measured-versus-
estimated flag, and calculated outputs.
 History record: water, electricity, carbon, overall score, and category breakdowns over time.
 Goal record: target action, metric, target value, start date, end date, status, and completion 
result.
 Achievement record: achievement type, date earned, and evidence or trigger.
 Streak record: cadence, current streak, best streak, and last valid check-in.
 Public profile settings: exact fields that the user has allowed to be shown publicly.
 Story consent and story record: separate from general public-account consent.
 Uploaded bill record: private file reference, extraction result, user-corrected values, and 
validation status.
21. Dashboard Output Requirements
The dashboard should summarize results in a way that is useful both immediately and over time. It 
should include:
 Total water consumption.
 Total electricity consumption.
 Total carbon footprint.
 Category breakdowns and largest contributors.
 National and other relevant benchmark comparisons where supported.
 Change from previous period and change since joining.
 Separate historical graphs for each metric.
 Per day, week, month, or year display selector.
 Relatable equivalence comparisons.
 AI-generated interpretation.
 Specific weekly goals.
 Progress toward active goals.
 Current streak and recent achievements.
 Reactive mascot and nature visuals linked to current impact.

Appendix A. Non-Negotiable Implementation Rules
1. Ask only relevant and meaningful questions.
2. Prefer measured values over estimates.
3. Use the same underlying schema and calculation engine for questionnaire and chat input.
4. Use an LLM API for conversational collection, analysis, and planning; use a multimodal model or 
document-extraction API for bill images.
5. Never let the LLM perform the authoritative footprint arithmetic.
6. Require users to review AI-extracted bill data before calculation.
7. Keep uploaded bills, raw household data, and private chats private regardless of public profile 
status.
8. Require explicit consent for public profiles and separate consent for featured stories.
9. Show exact metrics together with context, trends, and relatable comparisons.
10. Provide specific weekly goals and allow the user to discuss and revise plans through the AI 
planner.
11. Support weekly or monthly reminders and corresponding streaks.
12. Use the light blue, green, white, and grey visual theme consistently.
13. Make the mascot and environmental wallpaper react gradually to the user's impact relative to 
appropriate benchmarks.
14. Keep the tone friendly and optimistic while remaining honest about poor performance and 
uncertainty.
